Your First 2 Minutes
With a New Patient

How to start every consultation with confidence — so the patient trusts you before the exam even begins.
Why the First 2 Minutes Matter

A patient decides whether they trust you in the first 120 seconds. Not during the exam. Not during the presentation. Right now, in the opening moment.

If you walk in nervous, talking fast, trying to impress them — they feel it. If you walk in calm, clear, and in control — they feel that too.

The goal is simple: set the tone for the entire visit in 2 minutes.

Do This Walk in slowly. Make eye contact. Sit down (don't stand over them). Tell them exactly what's going to happen today before you touch a single thing.
Don't Do This Don't rush in. Don't start with small talk about the weather. Don't immediately ask "so what brings you in?" before introducing the structure of the visit.
The Opening Script
Say This (word for word) "Before we get started, I want to tell you exactly what today looks like. My job isn't to treat your symptoms — it's to figure out what's actually going on. I'm going to listen to your story, run some tests, and then take the results home tonight to put together a clear picture. Tomorrow, I'll walk you through everything I found and we'll talk about whether I can help. Sound good?"

This script does 3 things: (1) It tells them you're thorough, not rushed. (2) It removes the pressure to "sell" anything today. (3) It positions tomorrow as the important conversation, which builds anticipation.

Two Rules for the First 2 Minutes

Rule 1: Speak Less

The less you say, the more confident you appear. If you're filling every silence with words, you're signaling uncertainty. Let there be pauses. They're not awkward — they're powerful.

Rule 2: Move Slower

Your pace sets the pace of the room. If you rush, they feel rushed. If you're calm and steady, they calm down too. This is especially important with anxious patients.

The Key Insight: Patients aren't looking for a friend — they're looking for someone who knows what they're doing. You show that through calm, clarity, and structure. The opening script gives them all three in under 30 seconds.

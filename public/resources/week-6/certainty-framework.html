Why Patients Hesitate

When a patient hesitates, it's almost never about the money. It's about uncertainty. They're not sure if it will work. They're not sure if they need that many visits. They're not sure if YOU are sure.

Your job isn't to convince them. It's to be so clear about what you found and what it requires that saying yes feels like the obvious choice.

The #1 mistake: When a patient hesitates, most doctors start talking more — explaining more, justifying more, offering discounts. This makes it worse. The more you explain, the less certain you look.

Instead: Stay quiet. Let them process. Your silence says "I'm confident in this recommendation and I'll wait while you think."

3 Scripts for Common Hesitations

1. "I need to think about it."

Say This "I completely understand. This is an important decision. Can I ask — what specifically are you thinking about? Is it the time commitment, the investment, or something else? I want to make sure I've answered all your questions."

This gets to the real objection. Usually it's one specific thing — not a general "no."

2. "That's a lot of visits."

Say This "I hear you. Here's why I'm recommending this frequency: your scans show [specific finding]. To correct that pattern, your body needs consistent input — similar to how physical therapy works. If we do fewer visits, we risk the problem coming back and starting over. I'd rather get it right the first time."

Always tie the recommendation back to their specific results. "Because your scans show..." is more powerful than "because that's what we recommend."

3. "Can I do fewer visits per week?"

Say This "I wouldn't recommend that, and here's why. At [fewer visits], we won't see the change fast enough and you'll end up spending more time and money in the long run. I'd rather give you the best shot at a real result. If after 4 weeks we're ahead of schedule, we can always adjust."

Don't negotiate the plan. But give them a milestone — "if after 4 weeks we're ahead, we adjust." That's reasonable and reassuring.

The Key Insight: Hesitation is not rejection. It's a request for more clarity. Answer their specific concern, stay calm, and let them decide. If you've explained it clearly and they still say no — that's OK. Not every patient is the right fit.

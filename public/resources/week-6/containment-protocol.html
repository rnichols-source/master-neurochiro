Training Your Team
to Stay Consistent

How to get your staff saying and doing the right things — even when you're not in the room.
The Problem

You've built great systems. You know exactly what to say to patients. But when your CA or front desk handles a call, they wing it. When you're in the back adjusting, the front desk is making promises you didn't approve or forgetting to reschedule a missed visit.

Your team doesn't need motivation. They need scripts and checklists.

3 Things to Standardize This Week
1. The Phone Script Write out exactly what your front desk says when a new patient calls. Word for word. Post it next to the phone. "Thank you for calling [Clinic]. This is [Name]. How can I help you today?" Then: collect name, concern, schedule the visit.
2. The Missed Visit Follow-Up When a patient no-shows, your CA texts within 4 hours: "Hi [Name], we missed you today! Want me to reschedule for tomorrow?" No judgment, just a warm nudge. Print the template and post it.
3. The Checkout Process After every visit: confirm next appointment, collect payment, and say one specific thing: "Great visit today — we'll see you [day] at [time]." No "see you whenever." Specific day, specific time, every time.

How to Roll This Out

Don't hand your team a 10-page manual. Give them one script this week. Practice it together for 5 minutes at the morning huddle. Once they've got it, add the next one. One script per week = a fully trained team in 3 weeks.

The Morning Huddle Script
5 Minutes Every Morning "Good morning team. Today we have [X] patients, including [X] new patients. [Name] has their re-exam today — let's make sure we have their comparison scans ready. Any missed visits from yesterday that need follow-up? OK — let's have a great day."

That's it. 5 minutes. Every morning. This single ritual improves collections by 20-40% because it keeps the team focused and accountable.

The Key Insight: Your team will perform exactly as well as the systems you give them. No system = inconsistent results. A simple script posted on the wall beats a 3-hour training session every time. Give them the words. Practice once. Then hold them to it.

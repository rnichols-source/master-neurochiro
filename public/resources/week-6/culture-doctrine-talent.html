Hiring and Keeping
Good People

How to find, onboard, and retain team members who care as much as you do.
Hiring: What to Look For

You can train skills. You can't train attitude. When hiring for a chiropractic office, prioritize these 3 traits over experience:

1. Warmth Do they smile naturally? Do they make you feel comfortable in the interview? Patients will feel the same thing.
2. Reliability Did they show up on time? Did they follow up after the interview? Small signals predict big patterns.
3. Coachability Give them feedback in the interview: "Try saying it this way." Do they adapt, or do they get defensive? You need adapters.

The Interview Question That Reveals Everything

Ask This "Tell me about a time you made a mistake at work and how you handled it."

People who own their mistakes and describe what they learned are the ones you want. People who blame others or can't think of an example are red flags.

Trial Day

Before hiring, have them spend a half day in the office. Watch how they interact with patients, how they handle downtime, and whether they ask questions. A trial day tells you more than 10 interviews.

Keeping Good People
Pay Fairly If you're paying below market, your best people will leave. Research what CAs and front desk staff make in your area and match or beat it. The cost of replacing someone is 3-6 months of their salary.
Give Feedback Weekly Don't wait for annual reviews. A 2-minute check-in every week ("here's what you did great, here's one thing to work on") keeps people growing and engaged.
Celebrate Wins When the team hits a goal, acknowledge it. It doesn't have to be a bonus — a genuine "great job this week, I noticed [specific thing]" goes a long way.
Give Them Ownership People stay when they feel like they own something. Give your CA ownership of the scheduling system. Let your front desk own the patient welcome process. Ownership creates pride.

The Key Insight: People don't quit jobs — they quit bosses. If you give clear expectations, regular feedback, and genuine appreciation, your team will stay. If you micromanage, ignore them, or only give feedback when something goes wrong, they'll leave — and your best people will leave first.

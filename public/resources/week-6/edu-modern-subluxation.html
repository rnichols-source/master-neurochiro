Explaining What's Wrong
In Patient Language

How to describe spinal dysfunction without using words your patients don't understand.
The Problem With Clinical Language

When you say "subluxation" or "vertebral dysfunction," patients hear noise. They don't know what it means, and they won't ask because they don't want to look stupid. So they nod, pretend to understand, and leave confused.

The fix: Use analogies they already understand. Here are 3 that work every time.

Don't Say This"You have a C5-C6 subluxation causing nerve interference in the brachial plexus."
Say This Instead"Part of your spine is stuck in a stressed position. It's putting pressure on the nerves that control your arm and hand. That's why you're getting the tingling."
3 Analogies That Patients Understand
The Circuit Breaker"Think of your spine like the circuit breaker box in your house. When a breaker trips, the lights in one room go out. Your spine works the same way — when a bone shifts and puts pressure on a nerve, the area that nerve controls stops working properly."
The Kinked Hose"Imagine a garden hose with a kink in it. The water still flows, but not at full pressure. Your nerves are like that hose — when there's pressure from a misalignment, the signals don't get through clearly."
The Check Engine Light"Your pain is like a check engine light. It's telling you something is wrong underneath. I can help with the pain, but more importantly, I want to fix what's causing the light to come on in the first place."
When Showing Scans"See this area here? [Point to the finding.] This part of your spine is stuck in a stress pattern. It's been like this long enough that your body adapted to it — that's why you didn't feel it until now. The good news is, we can work with this."

For the Doctor: You don't need to dumb down your clinical knowledge. You need to translate it. The patient doesn't care about the anatomy — they care about what it means for their life. Connect every finding to their symptoms and their daily activities.

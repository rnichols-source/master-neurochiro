Handling Flare-Ups
During Care

What to say when a patient has a bad day mid-plan — so they don't panic and quit.
Why Flare-Ups Happen (and Why They're Normal)

Around visit 5-10, many patients experience a flare-up — a day where they feel worse than they did last week. They call, worried, and say: "I think the adjustments made it worse."

This is one of the most critical moments in the care plan. If you handle it well, they stay and complete care. If you panic or get defensive, you lose them.

Why It Happens As the body starts changing its patterns, there's a transition period where old compensation patterns break down before new ones stabilize. It's like being sore after starting a new workout — it means something is changing, not that something is wrong.
The 3-Step Flare-Up Response

Step 1: Don't Panic — Normalize It

Say This "I'm glad you called. What you're experiencing is actually very common around this point in the plan. Your body is in the middle of changing a pattern it's been stuck in for [months/years]. That transition can feel uncomfortable for a day or two. It's not a setback — it's actually a sign of progress."

Step 2: Validate Their Concern

Say This "I understand it's frustrating to feel worse when you've been doing so well. That's a completely reasonable reaction. Here's what I want you to know — if this were actually getting worse, we'd see it on your scans. At your next visit, I'll double-check everything. But based on where you are in the plan, this is expected."

Step 3: Give Them Something to Do

Say This "For the next 24-48 hours, use ice for 15 minutes on / 15 off, stay hydrated, and move gently — a short walk is perfect. Come in for your next visit as planned. If you're still feeling off by then, we'll adjust our approach. But I expect you'll feel a lot better by tomorrow."

The Key Insight: The difference between losing a patient and keeping one often comes down to a single phone call. If you told them about flare-ups on Day 3 ("some days you'll feel great, some you won't"), they'll call you calm instead of panicked. If you didn't, they'll call you scared. Either way — normalize it, validate it, and give them a simple action step.

Prevention: What to Say on Day 3
Plant the Seed Early "As your body adjusts, you might have a day or two where you feel a little sore or different. That's completely normal — it means things are changing. If it happens, just call us and we'll talk you through it. It's part of the process."

Handling "I Need
to Think About It"

The most common objection in chiropractic — and exactly how to respond without being pushy.
Why They Say It

When a patient says "I need to think about it," they're not asking for more time. They're telling you one of two things:

1. They're not sure your plan will work. They heard the recommendation but aren't convinced it's the right solution for them.

2. They're worried about the money. They want the care but aren't sure they can afford it or justify the investment.

Your job is to figure out which one it is — without guessing.

The Common Mistake Most doctors hear "I need to think about it" and say "OK, take your time" — then the patient walks out and never comes back. You just lost a $3,000-$5,000 case because you didn't ask one follow-up question.
The Better Approach Don't argue. Don't discount. Just ask one simple question to find out what's actually holding them back.
The Script — 3 Steps

Step 1: Acknowledge (Don't Resist)

Say This "I completely understand. This is an important decision and I want you to feel good about it."

This disarms them. They expect you to push back. When you don't, they relax.

Step 2: Ask the Real Question

Say This "Can I ask — when you say you need to think about it, is it that you're not sure this is the right approach for you? Or is it more about the financial side? I just want to make sure I've answered all your questions."

Now they have to tell you the real reason. And once you know the real reason, you can address it specifically.

Step 3: Address What They Actually Said

If it's the plan:

Say This "That's fair. Let me show you one more time what your scans revealed and why I'm recommending this specific plan. [Review the key finding briefly.] Does that make more sense?"

If it's the money:

Say This "I appreciate you being honest about that. We have a few payment options that can make this more manageable. Let me have [staff name] walk you through them — most patients find one that works."

The Key Insight: "I need to think about it" is never the real objection — it's a cover for a specific concern they haven't voiced yet. One follow-up question reveals it every time. And once you know what it is, you can usually solve it in 30 seconds.

Quick Reference
The 3-Step Formula 1. Acknowledge ("I understand") → 2. Ask ("Is it the plan or the investment?") → 3. Address the specific concern. Done. No pressure, no awkwardness.

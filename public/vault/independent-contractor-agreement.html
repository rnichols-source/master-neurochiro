When to Use an Independent Contractor vs. Employee

The distinction between an independent contractor (IC) and an employee is not a matter of preference — it is a legal determination governed by federal and state law. Misclassification exposes your practice to back taxes, penalties, interest, and potential litigation. Use this agreement only when the working relationship genuinely satisfies the criteria for independent contractor status.

Indicators the worker is an IC:

  • The worker controls when, where, and how the work is performed.
  • The worker provides their own tools, equipment, and supplies.
  • The worker serves (or is free to serve) multiple clients or businesses.
  • The worker has a genuine opportunity for profit or loss.
  • The relationship is project-based or defined by deliverables, not hours.
  • The worker is not integrated into the practice's regular operations in the same manner as an employee.

Indicators the worker is an employee:

  • You set their schedule, dictate how they perform tasks, or require attendance at staff meetings.
  • You provide all tools, equipment, and workspace.
  • The worker performs services exclusively for your practice.
  • You pay by the hour (rather than by project or deliverable) and the worker has no risk of economic loss.
  • You provide training on how to perform the work (not just practice-specific orientation).

The IRS 20-Factor Test — Simplified

The IRS uses a multi-factor analysis (commonly called the "20-Factor Test") grouped into three categories:

  1. Behavioral Control: Does the practice direct how the work is done? (Instructions, training, evaluation methods.) If yes, this points toward employee status.
  2. Financial Control: Does the worker have unreimbursed business expenses, investment in their own equipment, opportunity for profit/loss, and freedom to seek other work? If yes, this points toward IC status.
  3. Relationship Type: Is there a written contract? Are employee-type benefits provided? Is the relationship permanent or indefinite? Permanence and benefits point toward employee status.

No single factor is determinative. The totality of the relationship controls. When in doubt, file IRS Form SS-8 for a formal determination.

Misclassification Risks

Financial Exposure: If the IRS or your state labor department reclassifies a contractor as an employee, you may owe: (a) the employer's share of FICA taxes for the entire period of misclassification, plus interest; (b) federal and state penalties ranging from $50 to $500+ per misclassified worker per form; (c) unpaid overtime, benefits, and workers' compensation premiums; (d) legal fees and potential class-action liability if multiple workers are affected.

HIPAA Requirements for Contractors

Any contractor who will access, create, receive, maintain, or transmit Protected Health Information (PHI) on behalf of your practice is a Business Associate under HIPAA. You are required to:

  • Execute a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) before the contractor accesses any PHI.
  • Ensure the contractor implements administrative, physical, and technical safeguards.
  • Include breach notification obligations in the BAA.
  • Limit PHI access to the minimum necessary for the contractor's scope of work.
  • Retain the BAA for at least six (6) years after termination of the relationship.

This agreement contains a HIPAA/confidentiality section, but if the contractor will have significant PHI access, execute a standalone BAA as well (see Vault document: Business Associate Agreement).

6. Confidentiality and HIPAA Compliance

6.1 Confidential Information. 6.2 HIPAA Compliance. To the extent Contractor will access, use, create, receive, maintain, or transmit Protected Health Information ("PHI") as defined by the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 ("HIPAA"), as amended by the Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health Act ("HITECH"), and their implementing regulations (collectively, the "HIPAA Rules"), Contractor acknowledges and agrees that:

  • Contractor is a "Business Associate" as defined under the HIPAA Rules;
  • The Parties shall execute a separate Business Associate Agreement ("BAA") prior to Contractor's access to any PHI, which BAA is incorporated herein by reference;
  • Contractor shall implement and maintain appropriate administrative, physical, and technical safeguards to protect PHI in accordance with the HIPAA Security Rule;
  • Contractor shall use and disclose PHI only as permitted under the BAA and the HIPAA Rules, and only to the minimum extent necessary to perform the Services;
  • Contractor shall promptly report to Practice any use or disclosure of PHI not authorized by the BAA or the HIPAA Rules, including any Security Incident or Breach (as defined by the HIPAA Rules), within twenty-four (24) hours of discovery;
  • Contractor shall ensure that any subcontractors or agents who access PHI agree in writing to the same restrictions and conditions that apply to Contractor under the BAA.

6.3 Return of Information. Upon termination of this Agreement, Contractor shall return or destroy all Confidential Information and PHI in its possession, custody, or control, and shall certify such return or destruction in writing within fifteen (15) calendar days of termination.

6.4 Injunctive Relief. Contractor acknowledges that any breach of this Section 6 may cause irreparable harm to Practice for which monetary damages would be inadequate. Accordingly, Practice shall be entitled to seek injunctive and other equitable relief in addition to all other remedies available at law or in equity, without the necessity of posting a bond or proving actual damages.

13.2 Mediation. If the Dispute is not resolved through negotiation within the time period specified in Section 13.1, either Party may submit the Dispute to mediation administered by the American Arbitration Association ("AAA") / a mutually agreed-upon mediator in City, State. 13.3 Arbitration. If the Dispute is not resolved through mediation, either Party may submit the Dispute to binding arbitration administered by the AAA in accordance with its Commercial Arbitration Rules, conducted in City, State. The prevailing party shall be entitled to recover its reasonable attorneys' fees and costs from the non-prevailing party.

13.4 Injunctive Relief. Notwithstanding the foregoing, either Party may seek injunctive or other equitable relief from any court of competent jurisdiction to prevent irreparable harm pending the outcome of arbitration.

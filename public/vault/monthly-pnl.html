Monthly Profit & Loss Template

Chiropractic-Specific Financial Tracking for Practice Owners

Your Profit and Loss statement is the financial heartbeat of your practice. It tells you exactly where money is coming from, where it is going, and what is left at the end of the month. Without a clear P&L, you are guessing. With one, you are leading. This template is designed specifically for chiropractic practices, with categories that match how your money actually flows.

How to Fill Out Your P&L Monthly

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Set a date. Choose the same day each month to complete your P&L. The 5th of the following month works well, as it gives time for all transactions to clear. Put it on your calendar as a recurring appointment.
  2. Gather your data. You need: your bank statement, credit card statements, payroll reports, EHR collections report, and any cash logs. Having everything in front of you makes this a 30-minute task instead of a 3-hour hunt.
  3. Enter revenue first. Pull collections by category from your EHR and bank deposits. Use actual cash received, not billed amounts. This is a cash-basis P&L, which is the most useful for practice management.
  4. Enter direct costs. These are costs directly tied to delivering care: supplies, imaging costs, and lab fees. Subtract these from total revenue to get your gross profit.
  5. Enter operating expenses. Go line by line through each category. Use your bank and credit card statements to ensure nothing is missed. If an expense does not fit a listed category, put it in miscellaneous, but try to keep miscellaneous under 5% of total expenses.
  6. Calculate your key ratios. These ratios tell you if your practice is financially healthy. Compare them to the targets provided and to your own previous months to spot trends.
  7. Review and act. Your P&L is not just a report. It is a decision-making tool. If overhead is creeping up, identify why. If profit margin is shrinking, determine whether it is a revenue problem or a spending problem. Every number tells a story.

Key Financial Ratios

These ratios give you an instant health check on your practice finances. Track them monthly and watch for trends. A single month out of range is not cause for alarm. Three consecutive months trending the wrong direction is a signal to act.

"What Good Looks Like" Guide

Overhead Percentage — Target: Less than 55%

Your overhead percentage tells you how many cents of every dollar collected go to running the practice before the doctor gets paid. A 55% overhead means 45 cents of every dollar is available for doctor compensation, savings, and investment.

  • Under 50%: Excellent. Your practice is lean and efficient. Make sure you are not under-investing in growth (marketing, staff, equipment).
  • 50-55%: Good. This is the healthy range for a well-run practice.
  • 55-65%: Caution. Review your largest expense categories for savings opportunities. Often rent, payroll, or marketing are the culprits.
  • Over 65%: Critical. Your practice is not generating enough profit to sustain the doctor and reinvest in growth. Immediate review needed.

Profit Margin — Target: Greater than 40%

Profit margin is the ultimate score. It tells you what percentage of every dollar collected becomes profit (before doctor compensation in a sole proprietorship, or after doctor salary in a corporation).

  • Over 50%: Elite. You are running a highly profitable practice. Consider reinvesting in growth opportunities.
  • 40-50%: Strong. This is the target range for a healthy chiropractic practice.
  • 30-40%: Acceptable but improvable. Look for revenue growth opportunities and expense reduction.
  • Under 30%: Struggling. Either revenue is too low or expenses are too high. Often both. Requires immediate strategic attention.

Cost Per Visit

This number tells you the minimum you must collect per visit just to break even. Calculate it by dividing your total monthly expenses by your total monthly patient visits. If your cost per visit is $35 and you are collecting $65 per visit on average, you are making $30 in gross profit per visit. If your cost per visit exceeds your average collection per visit, you are losing money on every patient you see.

Revenue Per Visit

This measures the average amount you collect each time a patient walks through the door. It is influenced by your fee schedule, your payer mix (cash vs. insurance), your case types, and your ancillary services. Track this monthly. If it is declining, investigate whether insurance reimbursements are dropping, your cash patient percentage is changing, or you are discounting too aggressively.

Staff Cost Percentage — Target: Less than 25%

Staff is typically the second-largest expense after rent. This ratio tells you if your team is appropriately sized for your revenue. Include all staff costs: wages, payroll taxes, health insurance, retirement contributions, and any bonuses.

  • Under 20%: Lean team. Make sure you are not understaffed and burning out your current team.
  • 20-25%: Healthy range. Your team is appropriately sized for your production.
  • 25-30%: Watch zone. Either revenue needs to increase or you need to evaluate staffing levels.
  • Over 30%: Overstaffed relative to revenue. Review roles and responsibilities for redundancy or revenue growth needed to justify the team size.

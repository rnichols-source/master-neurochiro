Chiropractor's Guide — Non-Compete Enforceability

States That Ban or Severely Restrict Non-Competes

Non-compete enforceability varies dramatically by state. Before using this template, you must understand your state's current law. The following jurisdictions have enacted significant bans or restrictions as of 2025:

JurisdictionStatusKey Details
CaliforniaTotal BanNon-competes are void and unenforceable under Business & Professions Code § 16600. Employers cannot even require employees to sign them. As of January 1, 2024 (SB 699 and AB 1076), California also prohibits enforcement of non-competes signed in other states against California residents and requires notification to affected employees.
ColoradoRestrictedEffective August 10, 2022, non-competes are void unless for executive/management or professional staff earning at least the threshold salary (adjusted annually; approximately $123,750 for 2024). Non-solicitation restricted to those earning at least 60% of that threshold. Requires notice to employee.
MinnesotaTotal BanEffective July 1, 2023, non-competes are banned for all employees. Non-solicitation and confidentiality agreements remain enforceable. Applies to agreements entered into after the effective date.
North DakotaTotal BanNon-competes are generally void under N.D. Cent. Code § 9-08-06, with narrow exceptions for the sale of a business.
OklahomaTotal BanNon-competes are generally void under 15 O.S. § 219A, with limited exceptions for the sale of a business and dissolution of partnerships. Non-solicitation provisions may be enforceable.
Washington, D.C.Near-Total BanThe Ban on Non-Compete Agreements Amendment Act of 2020 (effective October 1, 2022) prohibits non-competes for nearly all employees. Narrow exception for medical specialists earning over $250,000.
OregonRestrictedNon-competes limited to 12 months maximum, only for employees earning above median family income, and employer must provide signed written copy within 30 days. Effective 2024 updates further tightened requirements.
Washington StateRestrictedRCW 49.62: Non-competes void for employees earning less than $116,593.18 (adjusted annually). Maximum duration 18 months. Must be disclosed by or before acceptance of employment.
IllinoisRestrictedEffective January 1, 2022: Non-competes prohibited for employees earning $75,000 or less (increasing to $90,000 by 2037). Non-solicitation prohibited for employees earning $45,000 or less. Requires 14-day review period and attorney consultation advisement.
MassachusettsRestrictedNon-competes limited to 12 months, require garden leave or other mutually-agreed consideration, and are prohibited for non-exempt employees, undergraduate/graduate students, and employees terminated without cause.
Federal Update (2024-2025): The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) issued a final rule in April 2024 that would have banned most non-competes nationwide. However, this rule was struck down by federal courts (Ryan LLC v. FTC, August 2024) and is not in effect. Monitor federal developments, as new legislation or rulemaking may emerge. State law remains the primary authority governing non-compete enforceability.

Three Pillars of an Enforceable Non-Compete

  1. Reasonable Time Restriction. Courts in most states will uphold restrictions of 1-2 years. Anything over 3 years is almost universally struck down. For chiropractic practices, 1-2 years is the sweet spot: long enough to protect the practice's patient relationships while short enough to survive judicial scrutiny.
  2. Reasonable Geographic Restriction. The restricted area must reflect the actual geographic reach of your practice. A 10-25 mile radius is generally reasonable for a chiropractic practice serving a local community. Rural practices may justify a wider radius; urban practices should use a narrower radius. Statewide restrictions are almost never upheld for a single-location practice.
  3. Adequate Consideration. The restricted party must receive something of value in exchange for accepting the restriction. For new employees, the job itself is usually sufficient consideration. For existing employees being asked to sign a new non-compete, additional consideration is required (a raise, bonus, promotion, additional PTO, or a specific payment). Failure to provide adequate consideration is one of the most common reasons non-competes are invalidated.

Common Mistakes That Make Non-Competes Unenforceable

  • Scope too broad. Restricting the associate from "any healthcare practice" rather than specifically "chiropractic practice" makes the restriction overbroad and courts will often void it entirely.
  • No independent consideration for existing employees. Asking a current employee to sign a non-compete without providing additional compensation or benefits beyond continued employment. Many states require new, independent consideration for mid-employment restrictive covenants.
  • Unreasonable geographic reach. A 50-mile radius around a suburban practice where 90% of patients live within 10 miles is unreasonable and invites judicial invalidation.
  • No blue pencil provision. Without a blue pencil clause, a court that finds any provision unreasonable may void the entire agreement rather than modifying the offending provision. Always include this provision.
  • Applying the restriction after termination without cause. Some states will not enforce non-competes when the employer terminated the employee without cause. Address this scenario explicitly.
  • Using a generic template from the internet without state-specific customization. Non-compete law is intensely state-specific. A provision that is enforceable in Texas may be void in California. Always have a local attorney review.
  • Failing to provide a copy to the employee. Several states now require that the employee receive a copy of the non-compete before or at the time of hire, with adequate time to review and consult an attorney.

2. Definitions

As used in this Agreement, the following terms shall have the meanings set forth below:

2.1 "Restricted Area" means the geographic area within a radius of __________ (e.g., 15) miles measured from each Practice Location at which Restricted Party provided services, performed duties, or had access to patient relationships at any time during the twelve (12) months preceding the termination of Restricted Party's relationship with Practice. As of the Effective Date, the Practice Locations subject to this restriction are:

  1. Primary Location: Address
  2. Satellite Location(s): Address(es), or "None"

2.2 "Restricted Period" means the period commencing on the date of termination or expiration of Restricted Party's employment, independent contractor, or partnership relationship with Practice (regardless of the reason for termination) and continuing for __________ (e.g., Two (2)) year(s) thereafter.

2.3 "Competitive Activity" means any of the following activities:

  1. The practice of chiropractic, including but not limited to the examination, diagnosis, treatment, or management of neuromusculoskeletal conditions through manual adjustments, spinal manipulation, or any other chiropractic technique;
  2. The ownership, operation, management, or control of a chiropractic practice, clinic, or office;
  3. Employment as a chiropractor, whether as an employee, independent contractor, partner, member, shareholder, or consultant, at any chiropractic practice, clinic, or office;
  4. The solicitation, treatment, or management of any patient of Practice; and
  5. Any other professional activity that directly competes with the chiropractic services offered by Practice.

2.4 "Confidential Information" means all non-public information relating to Practice's business, patients, and operations, including but not limited to: patient lists, patient records, patient contact information, referral source relationships, fee schedules, payer contracts, financial records, marketing strategies, clinical protocols, proprietary treatment methodologies, employee compensation data, business plans, and any information that Practice designates as confidential or that a reasonable person would understand to be confidential given the circumstances of disclosure.

2.5 "Patient of Practice" means any individual who received chiropractic services from Practice, or who had a scheduled appointment or active treatment plan with Practice, at any time during the twenty-four (24) months preceding the termination of Restricted Party's relationship with Practice.

3. Non-Competition Covenant

3.1 Non-Competition Obligation. During the Restricted Period and within the Restricted Area, Restricted Party shall not, directly or indirectly, whether as a principal, agent, employee, independent contractor, partner, member, shareholder, officer, director, consultant, advisor, or in any other capacity:

  1. Engage in any Competitive Activity;
  2. Own, manage, operate, control, finance, or participate in the ownership, management, operation, control, or financing of any entity engaged in Competitive Activity;
  3. Be employed by, serve as a consultant to, or render services for any entity engaged in Competitive Activity;
  4. Solicit, contact, or communicate with any Patient of Practice for the purpose of providing chiropractic services or inducing such patient to seek chiropractic care from any provider other than Practice;
  5. Accept or treat any Patient of Practice, regardless of whether the patient was solicited by Restricted Party; or
  6. Assist, advise, or encourage any other person or entity in engaging in any of the foregoing activities.

3.2 Indirect Competition. Restricted Party shall not circumvent the restrictions of this Section 3 by engaging in Competitive Activity through a spouse, family member, business associate, nominee, agent, or any other intermediary, whether as a disclosed or undisclosed principal.

3.3 Geographic Reasonableness. Restricted Party acknowledges that Practice draws patients from the geographic area described in the Restricted Area and that the geographic restriction set forth herein is reasonable and necessary to protect Practice's legitimate business interests. Restricted Party further acknowledges that Restricted Party is able to earn a livelihood in chiropractic practice outside the Restricted Area without undue hardship.

3.4 Temporal Reasonableness. Restricted Party acknowledges that the Restricted Period is reasonable given the nature of the patient-provider relationship in chiropractic care, the time required for Practice to establish new patient relationships following Restricted Party's departure, and the duration of Restricted Party's access to Confidential Information and patient relationships.

4. Scope of Restriction

4.1 Activities Specifically Prohibited. Without limiting the generality of Section 3, the following activities are expressly prohibited during the Restricted Period within the Restricted Area:

  1. Opening or Operating a Practice. Restricted Party shall not open, establish, acquire, or operate a chiropractic clinic, office, or practice of any kind, whether as a sole proprietor, partner, member of an LLC, shareholder of a professional corporation, or in any other capacity.
  2. Joining a Competing Practice. Restricted Party shall not accept employment or an independent contractor engagement with any other chiropractic practice, multi-disciplinary clinic that offers chiropractic services, hospital-based chiropractic department, or any other entity that provides chiropractic services.
  3. Patient Solicitation. Restricted Party shall not contact, solicit, or communicate with any Patient of Practice, by any means whatsoever (including but not limited to telephone, email, text message, social media, direct mail, or in-person contact), for the purpose of providing or offering to provide chiropractic services.
  4. Referral Source Solicitation. Restricted Party shall not contact, solicit, or communicate with any physician, attorney, or other referral source of Practice for the purpose of diverting referrals away from Practice or toward a competing chiropractic provider.
  5. Use of Confidential Information. Restricted Party shall not use any Confidential Information obtained during Restricted Party's relationship with Practice for the purpose of competing with Practice, soliciting patients or referral sources, or for any other purpose adverse to Practice's interests.
  6. Employee Recruitment. Restricted Party shall not recruit, solicit, hire, or attempt to hire any employee, independent contractor, or staff member of Practice, or encourage any such individual to terminate their relationship with Practice.

4.2 Social Media and Online Presence. Restricted Party shall not use social media, online advertising, or digital marketing to target or solicit patients or referral sources within the Restricted Area. Restricted Party shall remove or modify any online profile or listing (including but not limited to Google Business Profile, Yelp, Healthgrades, or personal website) that identifies Restricted Party as practicing chiropractic within the Restricted Area during the Restricted Period.

5. Permitted Activities

5.1 Notwithstanding the restrictions set forth in Sections 3 and 4, the following activities shall not constitute a violation of this Agreement:

  1. Practice Outside the Restricted Area. Restricted Party may engage in the practice of chiropractic at any location outside the Restricted Area without limitation.
  2. Teaching and Education. Restricted Party may serve as a faculty member, lecturer, or instructor at any accredited educational institution, including chiropractic colleges, provided that such activities do not involve the direct clinical treatment of patients within the Restricted Area.
  3. Non-Clinical Consulting. Restricted Party may provide non-clinical consulting services (such as practice management consulting, expert witness services, or insurance review services) that do not involve the direct examination, diagnosis, or treatment of chiropractic patients within the Restricted Area.
  4. Research and Publishing. Restricted Party may engage in clinical research and publish articles, books, or other scholarly works related to chiropractic science and practice.
  5. Volunteer Services. Restricted Party may provide volunteer chiropractic services at community health events, charitable organizations, religious organizations, or disaster relief efforts, provided such activities are truly volunteer in nature and do not serve as a means to establish a competing patient base within the Restricted Area.
  6. Employment in Non-Chiropractic Fields. Restricted Party may accept employment in any field other than chiropractic, including but not limited to other licensed healthcare professions for which Restricted Party holds a separate, independent license (e.g., acupuncture, massage therapy, physical therapy), provided that Restricted Party does not provide chiropractic services in connection with such employment.
  7. Passive Investment. Restricted Party may hold a passive investment interest of less than five percent (5%) in a publicly traded company that provides chiropractic services, provided Restricted Party does not participate in the management or operations of such company.

7. Remedies for Breach

7.1 Irreparable Harm. Restricted Party acknowledges and agrees that a breach of the restrictive covenants set forth in this Agreement would cause Practice immediate and irreparable harm that cannot be adequately compensated by monetary damages alone. Restricted Party further acknowledges that the harm caused by such breach would be difficult or impossible to quantify.

7.2 Injunctive Relief. In the event of a breach or threatened breach of this Agreement, Practice shall be entitled to seek and obtain temporary, preliminary, and permanent injunctive relief from any court of competent jurisdiction, without the necessity of proving actual damages and without the requirement of posting a bond or other security (or, if a bond is required by applicable law, a nominal bond). Such injunctive relief shall be in addition to, and not in lieu of, any other remedies available to Practice at law or in equity.

7.3 Extension of Restricted Period. If Restricted Party breaches any provision of this Agreement, the Restricted Period shall be automatically extended by the duration of the breach, so that Practice receives the full benefit of the bargained-for period of restriction. The extended Restricted Period shall commence upon the date Restricted Party ceases the breaching activity or the date of a court order enforcing this Agreement, whichever is later.

7.4 Liquidated Damages. In addition to injunctive relief, in the event of a breach of the non-competition covenant set forth in Section 3, Restricted Party shall pay Practice liquidated damages in the amount of $__________ (the "Liquidated Damages"). The Parties agree that this amount represents a reasonable estimate of the damages Practice would suffer from a breach of the non-competition covenant and is not intended as a penalty. Practice's acceptance of Liquidated Damages shall not waive Practice's right to seek injunctive relief or any other remedy available under this Agreement or applicable law.

7.5 Attorneys' Fees and Costs. In any action or proceeding arising out of or related to this Agreement, including any action to enforce the restrictive covenants herein, the prevailing Party shall be entitled to recover its reasonable attorneys' fees, court costs, expert witness fees, and all other expenses incurred in connection with such action, in addition to any other relief to which the prevailing Party may be entitled.

7.6 Cumulative Remedies. The remedies set forth in this Section 7 are cumulative and not exclusive. Practice may pursue any one or more of these remedies simultaneously or in any order, and the exercise of one remedy shall not constitute a waiver or election of remedies with respect to any other available remedy.

7.7 Forfeiture of Non-Compete Payment. If Practice has made or is making a Non-Compete Payment to Restricted Party pursuant to Section 6.1(c), and Restricted Party breaches this Agreement, Restricted Party's right to receive any further installments of the Non-Compete Payment shall be immediately forfeited, and Restricted Party shall repay to Practice all Non-Compete Payment amounts previously received within thirty (30) days of demand.

8. Severability

8.1 If any provision of this Agreement, or any portion thereof, is held to be invalid, illegal, or unenforceable by a court of competent jurisdiction or arbitrator, such invalidity, illegality, or unenforceability shall not affect the remaining provisions of this Agreement, which shall remain in full force and effect and shall be enforceable to the fullest extent permitted by law.

8.2 If any restrictive covenant or portion thereof is found to be unenforceable because it is too broad in scope, duration, or geographic area, the Parties intend that such covenant shall be reformed and enforced to the maximum extent permitted by applicable law, as set forth in Section 9 below.

8.3 Each restriction set forth in this Agreement (non-competition, non-solicitation of patients, non-solicitation of referral sources, non-solicitation of employees, and confidentiality) is a separate, independent, and severable obligation. The invalidity or unenforceability of any one restriction shall not affect the validity or enforceability of any other restriction.

9. Blue Pencil Provision

9.1 Judicial Modification. If any court of competent jurisdiction or arbitrator determines that any restrictive covenant set forth in this Agreement is unenforceable because it exceeds what is reasonable in duration, geographic scope, or breadth of restricted activities, the Parties expressly agree that the court or arbitrator shall have the authority to reform, modify, and enforce such covenant to the maximum extent that the court or arbitrator deems reasonable and enforceable under the circumstances, rather than rendering the covenant void and unenforceable in its entirety.

9.2 Intent of the Parties. It is the intent of the Parties that each restrictive covenant be enforced to the fullest extent permissible under applicable law. The Parties acknowledge that they have negotiated in good faith and that the restrictions set forth herein represent the Parties' best efforts to create reasonable, enforceable restrictions. However, to the extent a court or arbitrator finds any restriction excessive, the Parties prefer judicial modification over total invalidation.

9.3 Application. Without limiting the generality of this Section 9, if a court or arbitrator determines that:

  1. The Restricted Period is unreasonably long, the court or arbitrator may reduce it to the longest period that is reasonable;
  2. The Restricted Area is unreasonably large, the court or arbitrator may reduce it to the largest area that is reasonable;
  3. The scope of restricted Competitive Activity is unreasonably broad, the court or arbitrator may narrow it to the broadest scope that is reasonable; and
  4. Any other term or condition is unreasonable, the court or arbitrator may modify it to the extent necessary to make it reasonable and enforceable.

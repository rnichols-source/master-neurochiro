Comprehensive Partnership Agreement for Chiropractic Practices — With Valuation, Buy-Sell, and Exit Provisions

Practice Owner's Guide — Before You Sign Anything

Read this entire guide before completing the agreement. A partnership is a marriage — with money. Most practice partnerships fail not because the doctors were incompatible, but because they never discussed the hard questions upfront. This guide does not constitute legal advice. Engage a healthcare attorney and a CPA before executing this agreement.

5 Questions to Answer Before ANY Partnership

  1. What happens when one of us wants out? This is the single most important question. If you cannot agree on exit terms before you enter the partnership, you will destroy the practice when one of you leaves. The buy-sell provisions in Section 8 address this — but you must negotiate them now, not later.
  2. Who makes what decisions? Day-to-day clinical decisions are one thing. Signing a ten-year lease, hiring an associate, or adding a new service line are entirely different. Define the threshold. If you cannot agree on governance, you do not have a partnership — you have a conflict waiting to happen.
  3. How do we split money — and how do we handle lean months? Equal splits feel fair until one partner produces 70% of the revenue. Define whether compensation is production-based, equal, or hybrid. Define what happens when the practice has a losing month. Define draw limits.
  4. What are each partner's minimum commitments? Hours per week, vacation limits, CE requirements, community involvement. Put it in writing. Resentment builds when one partner works 50 hours and the other works 30.
  5. What is the practice worth — and how will we measure it? Agree on a valuation formula now, while both parties are rational and cooperative. Attempting to value a practice during a contentious departure is expensive, adversarial, and destructive.

Common Partnership Structures in Chiropractic

Structure How It Works Best For
50/50 Equal Partnership Both partners contribute equally, share profits and losses equally, and have equal voting rights. Simple and symmetrical. Two doctors starting a practice together from scratch, or a seasoned doctor bringing in an equally experienced partner.
60/40 or 70/30 Senior/Junior Founding partner retains majority ownership and decision-making authority. Junior partner earns a path to equity over time. Profit split may mirror ownership or be production-based. Established practice bringing in an associate with a buy-in path. The senior partner has built the patient base, systems, and brand.
Graduated Buy-In Junior partner starts as an associate (employee or IC), then purchases ownership incrementally over 3-7 years. Ownership percentage increases as buy-in payments are made. Risk-averse transitions. Allows both parties to test compatibility before full commitment. Provides the senior partner with liquidity over time.

How to Value a Chiropractic Practice

There is no single "correct" valuation method. The right approach depends on the practice's size, profitability, location, and the nature of the transaction. Here are the three most common methods:

Method 1: Multiple of Collections

The most common method for chiropractic practices. Take the practice's trailing 12-month gross collections and multiply by a factor, typically between 0.50 and 0.85 for chiropractic.

Example: A practice collected $850,000 in the trailing 12 months. Using a 0.75 multiplier: $850,000 x 0.75 = $637,500 estimated practice value. The multiplier depends on profitability, payer mix, location, transferability of patients, condition of equipment, and lease terms.

Method 2: Asset-Based Valuation

Sum of tangible assets (equipment, furniture, inventory at fair market value) plus intangible assets (goodwill, patient records, brand, systems). Goodwill is typically the largest component and is often calculated as 1-3 years of net income attributable to the practice (not the doctor personally).

Example: Equipment FMV: $120,000. Furniture/fixtures: $25,000. Inventory: $8,000. Goodwill (2x net income of $180,000): $360,000. Total: $513,000.

Method 3: Hybrid / Weighted Average

Average the results of multiple methods, sometimes weighting one more heavily. This is the most defensible approach and the one most often used in formal appraisals.

Example: Collections method yields $637,500. Asset-based yields $513,000. Weighted average (60% collections / 40% asset): ($637,500 x 0.60) + ($513,000 x 0.40) = $382,500 + $205,200 = $587,700. This figure becomes the basis for buy-in negotiations.

Exit Strategy Planning — From Day One

The time to plan your exit is the day you sign the partnership agreement. Consider:

  • Mandatory buy-sell insurance: Key person life insurance and disability buy-out insurance fund the agreement automatically if a partner dies or becomes disabled. Without insurance, the surviving partner may not have the liquidity to buy out the deceased partner's estate — leaving the estate as an unwilling co-owner.
  • Valuation updates: Agree to re-value the practice annually or bi-annually. A valuation from five years ago is useless and will be contested.
  • Non-compete enforceability: If the departing partner can open across the street, the buy-out is worthless. Ensure the non-compete is reasonable and enforceable in your state.
  • Patient transition: Patients chose a doctor, not a business entity. A 60-90 day transition period with formal introductions to the remaining partner protects revenue.
  • Lease assignment: If the practice leases its space, can the remaining partner assume the lease? Is the landlord's consent required? Address this before you need it.

8. Buy-Sell Provisions

8.1 Triggering Events. A "Triggering Event" occurs upon any of the following:

  1. Voluntary Withdrawal: A Partner provides written notice of intent to withdraw from the Partnership. Minimum notice period: 90 / 120 / 180 calendar days.
  2. Death: The death of a Partner.
  3. Disability: A Partner's inability, due to physical or mental illness, injury, or incapacity, to perform his or her material duties under this Agreement for a period of 90 / 120 / 180 consecutive calendar days, or for 120 / 180 cumulative days within any twelve (12) month period, as certified by a licensed physician selected by the remaining Partner(s) and reasonably acceptable to the disabled Partner (or his or her representative).
  4. Retirement: A Partner elects to retire from the active practice of chiropractic, upon not less than 180 / 365 calendar days' prior written notice.
  5. Breach: A Partner commits a material breach of this Agreement that remains uncured for thirty (30) calendar days after written notice, including but not limited to: violation of non-compete provisions, misappropriation of Partnership funds, conviction of a felony, loss of professional licensure, or repeated failure to meet minimum time commitments.
  6. Bankruptcy: A Partner files a voluntary petition in bankruptcy, is the subject of an involuntary petition in bankruptcy that is not dismissed within sixty (60) days, makes an assignment for the benefit of creditors, or has a receiver appointed for his or her assets.

8.2 Valuation Method. Upon the occurrence of a Triggering Event, the value of the departing Partner's interest (the "Buyout Price") shall be determined as follows:

Primary Valuation Formula:

Buyout Price = 75% of the Partnership's trailing twelve (12) month gross collections, multiplied by the departing Partner's ownership percentage at the time of the Triggering Event.

Example: If trailing 12-month collections are $1,200,000 and the departing Partner holds 40% ownership: $1,200,000 x 0.75 x 0.40 = $360,000 Buyout Price.

The detailed valuation methodology, including adjustments and alternative methods, is set forth in Exhibit C.

8.2.1 Adjustments to Buyout Price. The Buyout Price shall be adjusted as follows:

  • Accounts Receivable: The departing Partner shall receive his or her ownership percentage of accounts receivable outstanding as of the Triggering Event date, collected within 120 / 180 days thereafter, net of collection costs;
  • Liabilities: The Buyout Price shall be reduced by the departing Partner's proportionate share of all outstanding Partnership liabilities as of the Triggering Event date;
  • Capital Account: Any positive balance in the departing Partner's capital account in excess of the Buyout Price shall be paid in addition to the Buyout Price; any negative balance shall be deducted;
  • Breach Discount: If the Triggering Event is a breach under Section 8.1(e), the Buyout Price shall be reduced by 15 / 20 / 25%.

8.2.2 Independent Appraisal. If either Party disputes the Buyout Price calculated under the formula, either Party may request an independent appraisal by a qualified practice valuation professional. Each Party shall select one appraiser, and the two appraisers shall select a third. The average of the three appraisals shall be the Buyout Price. The costs of the appraisal shall be borne equally by the Parties.

8.3 Right of First Refusal. Upon the occurrence of a Triggering Event (other than death or disability), the remaining Partner(s) shall have the exclusive right and option to purchase the departing Partner's interest at the Buyout Price. The remaining Partner(s) shall have 60 / 90 calendar days from the determination of the Buyout Price to exercise this option by delivering written notice to the departing Partner. If the remaining Partner(s) do not exercise this option, the departing Partner may offer his or her interest to a third party, subject to the remaining Partner(s)' right to match any third-party offer within thirty (30) days.

☐ Lump Sum: The full Buyout Price shall be paid within 90 / 180 calendar days of the determination of the Buyout Price.

☐ Installments: The Buyout Price shall be paid in 36 / 48 / 60 equal monthly installments, commencing within 30 / 60 calendar days of the determination of the Buyout Price. The unpaid balance shall bear interest at X.X% per annum (or the applicable federal rate, whichever is higher). Payments shall be secured by a promissory note executed by the remaining Partner(s) in favor of the departing Partner.

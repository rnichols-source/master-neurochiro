Front Desk Phone Scripts

10 Word-for-Word Scripts for Every Call Your Practice Receives

The telephone is the front door of your practice. Every call is either the beginning of a lifelong patient relationship or a missed opportunity that walks to your competitor down the street. These scripts are not suggestions. They are carefully crafted word-for-word responses built on the psychology of trust, warmth, and competence. Print them. Post them at the front desk. Practice them until they feel natural.

Universal Phone Rules

  • Answer within 3 rings. Every ring past the third erodes caller confidence.
  • Smile before you answer. The caller can hear your facial expression in your voice.
  • Stand up for important calls. Standing improves vocal energy and confidence.
  • Use the caller's name at least twice. Nothing sounds as sweet as your own name.
  • Never put someone on hold without asking permission.
  • End every call by confirming the next step.

Script 1: Answering the Phone

The Psychology

The first 7 seconds of a phone call determine whether the caller feels they've reached the right place. Your greeting must communicate three things instantly: warmth, professionalism, and that they matter. A rushed or flat greeting tells the caller they are an interruption, not a priority.

"Thank you for calling [Practice Name], this is [Your Name], how can I help you today?"

If you recognize the caller:

"Thank you for calling [Practice Name], this is [Your Name]. Oh, hi Mrs. Johnson! How are you doing today?"
"[Practice Name], hold please." (Never answer and immediately place on hold.)

"Doctor's office." (Too generic. No warmth. No name.)

"Yeah?" or "Hello?" (Unprofessional. Makes the caller question if they dialed correctly.)
Warm, upbeat, and unhurried. Even if the office is chaotic behind you, the caller should hear calm confidence. Smile as you pick up the phone. Your voice should sound like you've been waiting for this call all day.

Script 2: New Patient Scheduling

The Psychology

A new patient calling your office has already overcome significant inertia. They are in pain, they've searched online, they've chosen your number. Your job is not to qualify them or interrogate them. Your job is to make scheduling feel easy, exciting, and inevitable. Every second of friction gives them a reason to hang up and call the next office on their list.

"I'd love to get you in with Dr. [Name]! Let me find a time that works perfectly for you. Are mornings or afternoons generally better?"

[After they respond]

"Great. I have [Day] at [Time] or [Day] at [Time]. Which of those works best for you?"

[After they choose]

"Perfect. I've got you down for [Day] at [Time] with Dr. [Name]. Can I get your full name? ... And a good phone number to reach you? ... And your email so I can send over your new patient paperwork ahead of time? That way you won't have to fill it out in the waiting room."

[After collecting info]

"Wonderful, [Patient Name]. You're all set for [Day] at [Time]. We're located at [Address]. Plan to arrive about 10 minutes early so we can get you settled in. Is there anything else I can help you with today? ... We look forward to seeing you!"
"What's your insurance?" (Never lead with insurance. It signals that payment matters more than their health.)

"What's wrong with you?" (Too clinical, too cold. If they volunteer their complaint, acknowledge it warmly.)

"Let me check if the doctor is taking new patients." (This creates doubt. If you have openings, you're taking new patients.)

"We're really busy this week." (Makes the caller feel like a burden.)
Enthusiastic and welcoming. You should sound genuinely excited that they called. Use phrases like "I'd love to" and "perfect" and "wonderful." The caller should feel like they just made the best decision of their week.

Script 3: "How Much Does It Cost?"

The Psychology

When a caller asks about cost, they are not being cheap. They are being responsible. They want to know they can afford care before they invest the emotional energy of hoping. Your goal is to validate their question, redirect to value, and get them in the door where the doctor can build enough trust and rapport that the investment feels worthwhile. Never quote a number that has no context.

"That's a great question, and I'm glad you asked. The truth is, every patient is different, so Dr. [Name] will customize a plan based on exactly what you need. Some patients need just a few visits, others need a longer plan. What I can tell you is that our initial visit, which includes a full exam and consultation, is [$ amount]. That gives Dr. [Name] everything needed to give you a clear picture of what's going on and exactly what it will take to get you feeling better. Would you like to get that scheduled?"

[If they press for more detail]

"I completely understand wanting to know upfront. Most of our patients find that their care plans range from [low range] to [high range] depending on the complexity, and we offer flexible payment options to make it comfortable. The best first step is that initial visit so Dr. [Name] can give you specific numbers based on your situation. Can I get you in this week?"
"It depends." (Too dismissive. Feels evasive.)

"We charge $75 per visit and most patients come 3 times a week for 12 weeks so that's about $2,700." (Too much too fast. No context, no value, just sticker shock.)

"We don't discuss pricing over the phone." (Feels like a bait-and-switch. The caller will not come in.)
Confident, transparent, and reassuring. Never sound apologetic about your fees. Never sound evasive. The caller should feel like you answered their question honestly without making them feel judged for asking.

Script 4: "Do You Take My Insurance?"

The Psychology

Insurance questions create anxiety for both the caller and the front desk. The caller fears being told "no" and having to start their search over. The front desk fears committing to coverage they cannot guarantee. The solution is to be helpful without making promises. Verify willingness to check, collect the information needed, and keep the momentum toward scheduling.

"We work with most major insurance plans, and I'd love to check on yours specifically. Do you have your insurance card handy? ... Great. What's the name of your insurance company? ... And is it a PPO, HMO, or do you know the plan type? ... Perfect. Let me make a note of that. What I'll do is verify your chiropractic benefits before your appointment so we can let you know exactly what's covered. In the meantime, let's go ahead and get you scheduled. Are mornings or afternoons better for you?"

[If they are not in-network]

"I want to be upfront with you. We are not currently in-network with [Insurance Name], but many of our patients with that plan still choose to see us because we can often submit claims on your behalf for out-of-network reimbursement. We also have very affordable self-pay options. Would you like to come in and let us show you how we can make it work?"
"No, we don't take that." (Conversation killer. Even if true, there are ways to still help the patient.)

"Yes, we take everything." (Overpromising leads to trust issues when the patient discovers their specific plan isn't covered.)

"You'll have to call your insurance company to find out." (Puts the burden on the caller. They called you for help.)
Helpful and proactive. The caller should feel like you are on their team, working to figure this out for them. Never sound annoyed by the question. Insurance is confusing for everyone, and the caller is looking for someone who makes it simple.

Script 5: Appointment Reminder Call (24 Hours Before)

The Psychology

Reminder calls reduce no-shows by up to 30%. But a robotic or impersonal reminder feels like a hassle. A warm, personal reminder feels like someone cares about the patient. The goal is to confirm the appointment, set expectations, and make the patient feel valued. This call should take less than 60 seconds.

"Hi, this is [Your Name] from [Practice Name]. I'm calling for [Patient Name]. I just wanted to give you a friendly reminder that you have an appointment with Dr. [Name] tomorrow, [Day], at [Time]. Please plan to arrive about 5 minutes early. If you have any questions before then, don't hesitate to call us at [Phone Number]. We look forward to seeing you tomorrow! Have a great evening."

[If you reach voicemail]

"Hi [Patient Name], this is [Your Name] from [Practice Name] with a friendly reminder about your appointment tomorrow, [Day], at [Time] with Dr. [Name]. If you need to make any changes, please give us a call at [Phone Number]. Otherwise, we'll see you tomorrow. Have a wonderful day!"
"This is a reminder that you have an appointment. Call us if you can't make it." (Cold, transactional, and invites cancellation.)

"We need you to confirm your appointment or it will be cancelled." (Threatening tone creates resentment, not compliance.)

"Just calling to make sure you're still coming." (Sounds doubtful and needy.)
Warm, brief, and cheerful. This is not a demand. It is a courtesy. The patient should feel cared for, not policed. Say the patient's name. Say the doctor's name. Make it personal.

Script 6: No-Show Follow-Up Call (Same Day)

The Psychology

A patient who no-shows is not a bad person. They are often embarrassed, overwhelmed, or forgot. The biggest mistake practices make is either ignoring the no-show (which signals that you don't care) or scolding the patient (which guarantees they never come back). Your call should communicate genuine concern for their well-being and make rescheduling feel easy and judgment-free.

"Hi [Patient Name], this is [Your Name] from [Practice Name]. We had you on the schedule today at [Time] and we noticed you weren't able to make it in. I just wanted to reach out and make sure everything is okay. We want to make sure you're doing well and that you stay on track with your care. When you get a chance, give us a call at [Phone Number] and we'll find a time that works better for you. We hope you're having a good day and we look forward to hearing from you!"

[If you reach the patient live]

"Hi [Patient Name], this is [Your Name] from [Practice Name]. We missed you at your appointment today and I just wanted to make sure you're okay. ... I completely understand. Life gets busy. Let's get you rescheduled so we can keep your progress going. I have [Day] at [Time] or [Day] at [Time]. Would either of those work for you?"
"You missed your appointment today." (Accusatory. Makes the patient defensive.)

"You need to give us 24 hours notice if you can't make it." (Policy lecture on a follow-up call destroys rapport.)

"Do you still want to continue your care?" (Passive-aggressive and gives the patient an easy exit.)

[Not calling at all.] (The worst option. Silence tells the patient you didn't notice or don't care.)
Concerned, warm, and completely non-judgmental. Imagine calling a friend who didn't show up to lunch. You would be worried, not angry. That is the tone. The patient should feel missed, not lectured.

Script 7: Cancellation Save Script

The Psychology

When a patient calls to cancel, there is almost always a reason behind the reason. "Something came up" often means "I'm not sure this is working." "I need to reschedule" sometimes means "I'm running out of money." Your job is to gently explore the real reason, address it if possible, and keep the patient in care. You will not save every cancellation, but you should save at least 40% of them.

"I understand, [Patient Name]. Can I ask what came up? ... I appreciate you sharing that with me. Dr. [Name] has been really encouraged by the progress you've been making, and I'd hate for you to lose momentum. Can we find another time this week that would work better for you? I have [Day] at [Time] available."

[If it's a financial concern]

"I completely understand, and I appreciate your honesty. Let me talk with our office manager about options. We want to make sure finances don't stand in the way of your health. Can I have someone give you a call back today to discuss some alternatives?"

[If they are unsure care is working]

"I hear you, and that's actually a really important conversation to have with Dr. [Name]. Sometimes progress feels slow on the inside even when things are improving on the scans and tests. Would you be open to coming in for a quick progress review so Dr. [Name] can show you exactly where you are? I can get you in for that this week."

[If they insist on cancelling]

"I completely respect that, [Patient Name]. I'm going to go ahead and open up that time slot. Just know that we're here for you whenever you're ready, and you don't need a referral to come back. I'll make a note in your chart. Take care of yourself."
"Okay, I'll cancel it." (Giving up without any attempt to understand or help.)

"You know you're going to lose your progress if you stop." (Fear-based guilt trip.)

"The doctor isn't going to be happy about this." (Unprofessional and intimidating.)

"Fine." (One word that communicates irritation and judgment.)
Empathetic, curious, and supportive. You are not arguing. You are not guilt-tripping. You are showing genuine care and offering solutions. The patient should feel heard and supported, even if they ultimately cancel.

Script 8: Handling a Complaint

The Psychology

A complaining patient is a gift. They are telling you what's wrong instead of simply leaving. Research shows that a complaint handled well creates a more loyal patient than one who never had a problem. The key is the L.A.S.T. framework: Listen, Apologize, Solve, Thank. Never defend, never deflect, never minimize. The patient needs to feel heard before they can hear your solution.

"I'm so sorry about that experience, [Patient Name]. That is not the standard we hold ourselves to, and I can absolutely understand why you'd be frustrated. Can you tell me a little more about what happened so I can make sure we address it? ...

[Listen fully without interrupting]

Thank you for sharing that with me. I want you to know that I take this seriously, and I'm going to make sure Dr. [Name] is aware of your concerns. Here's what I'd like to do: [specific action - reschedule, credit, speak with doctor, etc.]. Does that sound like a good next step? ...

And [Patient Name], I genuinely appreciate you telling us about this. It gives us a chance to make it right and to make sure it doesn't happen again. Your experience matters to us."
"Well, the doctor was running behind because we had an emergency." (Defending. The patient doesn't care why. They care that it happened.)

"That's never happened before." (Minimizing. Whether it's happened before is irrelevant to this patient.)

"There's nothing I can do about that." (Powerless and dismissive. There is always something you can do.)

"You should have said something at the time." (Blaming the patient for not complaining sooner.)
Sincere, calm, and solution-oriented. Lower your voice slightly. Slow your pace. The patient is likely emotional, and your calm is contagious. Never match their energy with defensiveness. Absorb it, validate it, and redirect it toward a solution.

Script 9: After-Hours Voicemail Greeting

The Psychology

Your after-hours voicemail is often the first impression for new patients who call outside business hours. It must accomplish four things: confirm they've reached the right place, provide hours, give an emergency option, and invite them to leave a message with the promise of a prompt callback. A poorly recorded or unprofessional voicemail costs you new patients every week.

"Thank you for calling [Practice Name]. Our office is currently closed. Our regular office hours are [Monday through Friday, 8:00 AM to 6:00 PM, or list your specific hours]. If you are experiencing a medical emergency, please hang up and dial 911.

If you are a current patient and need to reach Dr. [Name] for an urgent matter, please call [emergency number].

For all other calls, please leave your name, phone number, and a brief message, and we will return your call promptly on our next business day. If you'd like to schedule an appointment, you can also visit us online at [website].

Thank you for choosing [Practice Name]. We look forward to speaking with you soon."
"We're closed. Call back during business hours." (Rude, unhelpful, and will lose new patients immediately.)

"No one is available to take your call." (Makes the practice sound understaffed and unprofessional.)

[A greeting recorded on a cell phone with background noise.] (Invest in a properly recorded greeting. It is worth the effort.)

[A greeting that is more than 45 seconds long.] (Keep it concise. Callers will hang up if it rambles.)
Professional, warm, and clear. Record this in a quiet room. Speak slowly and enunciate. Play it back and listen as if you were a new patient hearing it for the first time. Would you leave a message? Would you feel confident about calling back? If not, re-record it.

Script 10: Warm Transfer to the Doctor

The Psychology

A warm transfer is the difference between a professional practice and a chaotic one. A cold transfer ("I'll transfer you") feels like being dumped. A warm transfer ("Let me introduce you to Dr. Smith, I've already filled her in") feels like VIP treatment. The 30 seconds you invest in a warm transfer builds enormous trust and prevents the patient from having to repeat themselves.

Step 1 - To the caller:
"[Patient Name], Dr. [Name] is going to be the best person to answer that question for you. Let me get the doctor on the line. I'm going to place you on a brief hold while I connect you. Is that okay? ... Great, one moment please."

Step 2 - To the doctor (on hold):
"Dr. [Name], I have [Patient Name] on line [number]. They're asking about [brief summary of the question or concern]. They seem [calm/concerned/in pain/frustrated]. Are you available to take the call?"

Step 3 - Connecting:
"[Patient Name], I have Dr. [Name] on the line for you. I've already let the doctor know what you're calling about so you won't have to repeat yourself. Dr. [Name], go ahead."
"Hold on, let me transfer you." [Click] (Cold transfer. The patient feels abandoned.)

"The doctor is busy. Can you call back?" (Dismissive. If the doctor is truly unavailable, take a detailed message and give a callback time.)

"I don't know, let me ask the doctor." (You are not the bottleneck. If you need the doctor, connect them directly.)
Confident and reassuring. The patient should feel like they are being personally escorted to the doctor, not passed off. Use the patient's name. Brief the doctor so the patient doesn't have to start from scratch. This small act of service creates enormous loyalty.

Implementation Guide

  1. Print all 10 scripts and place them in a binder at the front desk.
  2. Role-play each script during team training. Pair up and practice with a timer.
  3. Record yourself reading each script. Listen to the playback. Adjust tone and pacing.
  4. Mystery shop your own practice. Have a friend call and rate the experience. Do this quarterly.
  5. Review call handling during your weekly team meeting. Celebrate wins, coach misses.
  6. Update scripts with your practice name, doctor name, address, phone number, and website before posting.
  7. Customize for your voice. These scripts are frameworks. Adjust the exact wording so it feels natural coming from your team, but keep the structure and psychology intact.

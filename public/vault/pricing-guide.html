Care Plan Pricing Calculator Guide

How to Price Your Care Plans Profitably, Ethically, and Confidently

Pricing is one of the most uncomfortable topics for most chiropractors. You went to school to heal people, not to negotiate fees. But here is the truth: if your pricing does not support a profitable practice, you cannot serve anyone for long. This guide walks you through the math and strategy of pricing your care plans so that they are fair to the patient, profitable for the practice, and sustainable for the long term.

Step 1: Calculate Your True Cost Per Visit

Before you can set a price, you must know what it costs you to deliver a single visit. Most practice owners dramatically underestimate this number because they only think about supplies. Your true cost includes every dollar spent to keep the doors open, divided by the number of patients you see.

The Formula:

Cost Per Visit = Total Monthly Overhead / Total Monthly Patient Visits

Total Monthly Overhead includes: rent, utilities, staff payroll and taxes, insurance, marketing, software, supplies, equipment leases, professional dues, continuing education, and every other expense on your P&L except the doctor's compensation.

Step 2: Set Your Per-Visit Fee

Your per-visit fee must cover your cost per visit plus your desired profit per visit. This is not greed. This is math. Your profit funds your salary, your retirement, your reinvestment in better equipment, your ability to hire great people, and your capacity to serve your community for decades.

The Formula:

Per-Visit Fee = Cost Per Visit + Desired Profit Per Visit

Step 3: Pricing Strategies

Time-of-Service Discount

Offer a small discount (5-10%) to patients who pay at the time of service rather than being billed later. This reduces your accounts receivable, eliminates collection calls, and ensures you get paid. Present it as a benefit to the patient, not a loss to the practice.

How to Present It
"Mr. Jones, your fee for today's visit is $75. However, because you're paying at the time of service, we're happy to offer our time-of-service courtesy, which brings it to $68. Would you like to take advantage of that today?"

Pay-in-Full (PIF) Discount

Offer a 10-15% discount to patients who pay for their entire care plan upfront. This gives you immediate cash flow, eliminates the risk of the patient dropping out of care mid-plan, and simplifies your billing. The discount is justified by the value of guaranteed revenue and zero collection cost.

How to Present It
"Mrs. Smith, your care plan is 36 visits at $65 per visit, which totals $2,340. If you'd like to take advantage of our pay-in-full option, we offer a 15% courtesy, bringing the total to $1,990. That saves you $350 and means you never have to worry about payments for the duration of your care. Would you like to do that, or would you prefer one of our payment plan options?"

Family Plans

Offer a family rate that incentivizes multiple family members to receive care. Typically a 10-20% discount for additional family members beyond the first. This increases patient volume without proportionally increasing overhead (the room is already there, the staff is already there).

How to Present It
"Because your husband is already a patient here, we're happy to extend our family courtesy to you. Instead of $75 per visit, your visits will be $60 each. We love taking care of families because when the whole family is healthy, everyone benefits."

Payment Plan Structures

Payment Option How It Works Best For Practice Benefit
Pay in Full (PIF) Patient pays the entire care plan upfront, receives a 10-15% discount. Patients with savings, HSA/FSA funds, or who value simplicity. Roughly 20-30% of patients will choose PIF. Immediate cash flow. Zero collection risk. No monthly billing cost. Highest retention rate (patients who pay are patients who stay).
3-Pay Plan Total plan cost divided into 3 equal payments: first payment at signing, second at 30 days, third at 60 days. No discount (or 5% discount as incentive). Patients who cannot pay in full but can manage larger monthly payments. Roughly 30-40% of patients. Quick revenue collection. Low administrative burden. Only 3 transactions to track.
Monthly Auto-Draft Total plan cost divided into equal monthly payments, automatically charged to a credit card or bank account on file. Duration matches care plan length (4-6 months typical). Patients who need the lowest monthly payment. Roughly 20-30% of patients. Predictable monthly revenue. Automated collection eliminates staff follow-up. Card on file reduces missed payments.
CareCredit / Third-Party Financing Patient applies for a healthcare credit line through CareCredit, Lending Club, or similar. If approved, the practice receives full payment upfront. The patient makes payments to the finance company. Patients with larger care plans ($2,000+) who cannot manage payments to the practice directly. Also useful for uninsured patients. Practice receives full payment immediately. Zero collection risk. The finance company handles all billing. Note: the practice pays a merchant fee (typically 5-12% depending on the promotional period).

When to Raise Your Fees

The Rules of Fee Increases

  • Raise fees annually at minimum. Your expenses increase every year (rent escalations, payroll raises, supply cost inflation). If your fees stay flat, your profit margin shrinks every year. Most chiropractors have not raised fees in 3-5 years and wonder why they feel squeezed.
  • Target 3-5% increases annually. A 3-5% increase is below the threshold that patients notice or resist. On a $75 visit, a 5% increase is $3.75, bringing it to $78.75 (round to $79). No patient will leave your practice over $4 per visit.
  • Raise fees on January 1st. This aligns with insurance deductible resets and feels like a natural business cycle. Patients expect prices to increase annually across all services they use.
  • Communicate proactively. Do not surprise patients. Send a notice 30-60 days before the increase: "Effective January 1st, our fees will be updated to reflect increased costs of delivering care. Our commitment to providing you with the highest quality chiropractic care remains unchanged."
  • Never apologize for a fee increase. You are investing in better equipment, better training, better staff, and a better experience. A fee increase reflects the growing value you deliver.
  • Grandfather existing care plans. Patients currently on a care plan should keep their agreed-upon rate. The new fees apply to new care plans and renewals after the effective date.

Discount Policy Guide

When to Offer Discounts

Discount Type When It's Appropriate Typical Amount
Pay-in-Full Discount Always offer as a standard payment option. This is a legitimate business incentive for guaranteed revenue and zero collection cost. 10-15% off total care plan
Family Discount When a second or third family member begins care. Incentivizes household-level engagement and referrals. 10-20% off per-visit fee for additional family members
Hardship / Financial Assistance When a patient demonstrates genuine financial hardship and you want to make care accessible. Document the reason. Apply consistently. Never make it arbitrary. Up to 25-40% reduction, structured as a reduced fee schedule or reduced visit frequency, not as "free" care
Time-of-Service Discount Offered to patients who pay at the time of their visit rather than being billed later. Reduces A/R and collection costs. 5-10% per visit
Community Heroes / First Responder A practice-wide policy for active military, first responders, teachers, or other community groups you choose to honor. Builds goodwill and local reputation. 10-15% off per-visit fee

When NOT to Offer Discounts

  • When a patient is negotiating. If a patient says "I'll start care if you lower the price," do not negotiate. Your fees reflect the value of your training, your equipment, and your outcomes. Negotiating your fee trains the patient to view your care as a commodity. Instead, offer payment plan flexibility, not fee reductions.
  • "Just because." Random discounts without a policy create inconsistency, legal risk, and resentment when other patients find out. Every discount should be tied to a written policy that applies equally to all patients who qualify.
  • To compete with another practice's price. You are not the cheapest option and should never try to be. Compete on outcomes, experience, and expertise, never on price. The patient who chooses you based on price will leave you for a lower price.
  • When you feel guilty about your fees. Your fees are not a moral failing. They are the cost of delivering excellent care with a well-trained team in a professional environment. If you feel guilty about your fees, the issue is your confidence, not your pricing.
  • Under the table or off the books. Every discount must be documented. Offering undocumented fee reductions can create compliance issues, especially with insurance patients where you have a contractual obligation to collect patient responsibility amounts.

Insurance vs. Cash Considerations

Fee Schedule Setting

Your fee schedule should be set based on the value of your services, not on insurance reimbursement rates. Your full fee (also called your "usual and customary" or UCR fee) is the rate you charge to uninsured cash patients and the rate you submit to insurance companies. The insurance company then pays their allowed amount based on your contract with them.

Critical rule: Your UCR fee must be the same for all patients, regardless of insurance status. You cannot charge a cash patient $50 and submit $150 to insurance for the same service. This is considered fraud. Your fee schedule is your fee schedule. Discounts and adjustments are made from that fee schedule based on documented policies.

Dual Fee Schedule Issues

A "dual fee schedule" (charging different fees to different patients for the same service without a documented, uniformly applied policy) creates serious compliance risks. To stay compliant:

  • Maintain one fee schedule that applies to all patients.
  • Document any discount policies in writing and apply them consistently to all qualifying patients.
  • Cash/time-of-service discounts must be available to all patients equally, including insured patients for their patient responsibility portion (check your contracts first).
  • Never waive copays or deductibles for insured patients as an incentive. This is considered insurance fraud in most jurisdictions and can result in exclusion from insurance panels, fines, or criminal charges.

Compliance Best Practices

  • Keep your fee schedule updated annually and on file.
  • Document every discount given, including the reason and the policy it falls under.
  • If you participate in an insurance network, review your contract's provisions on fee schedules, discounts, and patient billing before implementing any new pricing strategy.
  • Consult with a healthcare compliance attorney if you have questions about your pricing structure. The cost of legal advice is far less than the cost of a compliance investigation.

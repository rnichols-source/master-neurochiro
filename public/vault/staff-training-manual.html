Section 3: Dress Code Standards

Your appearance is a direct reflection of our practice. Patients form impressions within seconds, and a professional appearance builds immediate trust and confidence.

Required Standards

  • Professional attire — Business casual at minimum. Scrubs are acceptable if they are clean, pressed, and in approved practice colors. No jeans, shorts, or athletic wear unless specifically approved for a practice event.
  • Clean and pressed — All clothing must be clean, free of wrinkles, stains, and wear. Iron or steam clothing before each shift.
  • Name tag — Your practice name tag must be worn visibly at all times during working hours. If your name tag is lost or damaged, notify the office manager immediately for a replacement.
  • Closed-toe shoes — Required for safety. Shoes must be clean and in good condition. No flip-flops, sandals, or open-toe footwear. Athletic shoes are acceptable if they are clean and professional in appearance.
  • Minimal jewelry — Small, professional jewelry is permitted. Avoid large dangling earrings, excessive bracelets, or anything that could interfere with patient care or get caught on equipment.
  • Grooming — Hair must be neat and pulled back if it falls past the shoulders. Nails should be clean and trimmed. Fragrances should be minimal, as patients may have sensitivities.
  • Tattoos and piercings — Visible tattoos should be covered when possible. Facial piercings beyond small studs should be discussed with the office manager.

Section 5: Phone Etiquette

All team members who answer the phone must follow the standards in the Front Desk Phone Scripts document. Key principles:

  • Answer within 3 rings, every time.
  • Smile before you speak. Your tone communicates more than your words.
  • Use the caller's name at least twice during the conversation.
  • Never put a caller on hold without asking permission first.
  • Use warm transfers. Never cold-transfer a patient.
  • End every call by confirming the next step and thanking the caller.
  • If you do not know the answer, say "That's a great question. Let me find out for you and call you right back." Then follow through within the hour.

Section 6: Patient Interaction Standards

Every patient interaction is an opportunity to build trust and loyalty. These standards apply to every team member, every interaction, every day.

The Five Standards

  1. Greet by name. Learn patients' names. Use them. When a patient hears their name, they feel known and valued. If you do not know their name, introduce yourself and ask.
  2. Stand up. When a patient approaches the front desk, stand up. This simple act communicates respect and attention. Never greet a patient while seated and looking at a screen.
  3. Smile. A genuine smile is the most powerful tool in your entire practice. It costs nothing and communicates everything. If you are having a bad day, smile anyway. Your patients did not cause your bad day and should not feel its effects.
  4. Escort, don't point. Never point a patient toward a room or area. Walk them there. Open the door. Make sure they are comfortable. Pointing says "figure it out yourself." Escorting says "you matter."
  5. Use positive language. Replace "I can't" with "What I can do is..." Replace "You need to" with "I'd recommend..." Replace "That's our policy" with "Here's how we can help." Language shapes experience.

Section 7: HIPAA Basics for Staff

What Is Protected Health Information (PHI)?

PHI is any information about a patient's health, treatment, or payment that can identify them. This includes: name, date of birth, address, phone number, email, Social Security number, insurance ID, medical records, appointment history, x-rays, billing records, and even the fact that they are a patient at your practice.

The Minimum Necessary Rule

Only access, use, or share the minimum amount of PHI needed to do your job. If you are scheduling an appointment, you do not need to read the patient's clinical notes. If you are billing a claim, you do not need to know what the patient discussed in their consultation. Access only what is necessary for your specific task.

Social Media Rules

  • Never post photos of patients, charts, or any identifiable health information on personal or practice social media without explicit written consent.
  • Never discuss patients by name (or with enough detail to identify them) on social media, even in private groups or messages.
  • Do not take photos or videos inside the practice that could capture patient information in the background (screens, charts, sign-in sheets).
  • If you see a patient in public, do not reference their care, their condition, or even the fact that they are a patient unless they bring it up first.

Breach Reporting

If you believe PHI has been accessed, used, or disclosed improperly, you must report it to the Privacy Officer (typically the office manager or doctor) immediately. Do not wait. Do not try to fix it yourself. Do not cover it up. Prompt reporting protects the patient, the practice, and you. Examples of breaches include:

  • Leaving a patient chart open on a computer screen in a public area
  • Discussing a patient's condition in the waiting room where others can hear
  • Sending patient information to the wrong email address or fax number
  • Losing a device (phone, laptop, USB drive) that contains patient information
  • A team member accessing records of a patient they are not treating or billing for

Consequences of Violations

HIPAA violations can result in disciplinary action up to and including termination, as well as personal fines ranging from $100 to $50,000 per violation. In cases of willful neglect or intentional disclosure, criminal penalties including jail time are possible. Take HIPAA seriously. It protects our patients and our practice.

Section 8: Insurance 101

Understanding insurance terminology is essential for every team member who interacts with patients about their coverage or financial responsibility. Here are the terms you need to know, explained simply.

Term What It Means Example
Deductible The amount the patient must pay out of pocket before insurance starts paying. Resets annually (usually January 1). A $1,500 deductible means the patient pays the first $1,500 of covered services themselves. After that, insurance begins to share the cost.
Copay A fixed dollar amount the patient pays at each visit, regardless of the total charge. A $30 copay means the patient pays $30 at each visit and insurance covers the rest of the allowed amount.
Coinsurance A percentage of the cost the patient pays after the deductible is met. Insurance pays the remaining percentage. 20% coinsurance means after the deductible is met, the patient pays 20% and insurance pays 80% of the allowed amount.
Pre-Authorization Approval from the insurance company before a service is performed. Required for some plans and some procedures. Without it, the claim may be denied. Some plans require pre-auth after the first 12 chiropractic visits. You must call and get approval before continuing care.
In-Network The provider has a contract with the insurance company and agrees to accept their fee schedule. Patient typically pays less. If you are in-network with Blue Cross, you accept their allowed amount and the patient's cost is based on that lower amount.
Out-of-Network The provider does not have a contract with the insurance company. The patient may pay more, and reimbursement rates may be lower. An out-of-network visit may be reimbursed at 50% instead of 80%, or may not be covered at all depending on the plan.
EOB (Explanation of Benefits) A statement from the insurance company showing what was billed, what was allowed, what they paid, and what the patient owes. The EOB shows: Billed $150, Allowed $95, Insurance Paid $76, Patient Owes $19.
Visit Limit The maximum number of chiropractic visits covered per year under the patient's plan. A plan with a 30-visit limit means insurance will cover up to 30 chiropractic visits per calendar year. After that, the patient pays the full fee.

Section 10: Progressive Discipline

Our goal is to help every team member succeed. When performance or behavior issues arise, we follow a progressive discipline process that is fair, consistent, and documented.

Step Action Documentation Typical Situations
Step 1: Verbal Warning Private conversation with direct supervisor. Clear description of the issue, expected behavior, and support offered. Employee acknowledges understanding. Supervisor notes the conversation with date and topic in the employee file. No formal write-up. First occurrence of tardiness, minor dress code violation, minor procedural error, first instance of unprofessional communication.
Step 2: Written Warning Formal meeting with supervisor and office manager. Written documentation of the issue, prior verbal warning, expected improvement, timeline for improvement, and consequences of continued issues. Written warning signed by employee, supervisor, and office manager. Copy placed in employee file. Copy given to employee. Repeated tardiness, repeated procedural errors, continued dress code violations, customer complaints about attitude, failure to follow protocols after verbal warning.
Step 3: Final Written Warning Formal meeting with supervisor, office manager, and doctor/owner. Clear statement that this is the final warning. Specific improvement required with deadline. Any further issues will result in termination. Final written warning signed by all parties. Improvement plan with specific milestones and check-in dates. Copy in file and to employee. Continued issues after written warning, moderate policy violations, patient care concerns, insubordination, attendance pattern issues.
Step 4: Termination Meeting with doctor/owner and office manager. Clear, compassionate communication of the decision. Collection of keys, badge, and equipment. Final paycheck processed per state law. Termination letter with reason, effective date, and final pay information. All prior warnings referenced. Exit checklist completed. Failure to improve after final warning, or immediate termination for gross misconduct (theft, HIPAA violation, violence, substance abuse on the job, patient endangerment).

Note: Certain offenses may warrant immediate termination without progressive steps. These include but are not limited to: theft, intentional HIPAA violations, violence or threats, working under the influence, falsifying records, and patient endangerment.

Section 11: Emergency Procedures

Medical Emergency

  1. Stay calm. Your composure will help the patient and the team.
  2. Call 911 immediately if the patient is unconscious, not breathing, experiencing chest pain, having a seizure, or showing signs of stroke (face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty).
  3. Notify the doctor immediately. If the doctor is with another patient, interrupt.
  4. Clear the area. Move other patients and non-essential staff away from the scene.
  5. Begin first aid as trained (CPR, AED, bleeding control) until EMS arrives.
  6. Assign a team member to meet EMS at the front door and guide them to the patient.
  7. Assign a team member to call the patient's emergency contact.
  8. Document the incident thoroughly after the emergency is resolved. Include times, actions taken, and EMS information.
  9. Debrief with the team after the event. Discuss what went well, what could improve, and provide emotional support.

Fire

  1. Pull the fire alarm if one is available.
  2. Call 911.
  3. Evacuate all patients and staff using the nearest exit. Assist any patients who need help moving. Do not use elevators.
  4. Account for everyone at the designated meeting point outside the building: Your designated meeting point
  5. Do not re-enter the building until cleared by the fire department.
  6. If safe, attempt to contain a small fire with a fire extinguisher (PASS method: Pull pin, Aim low, Squeeze handle, Sweep side to side). Do not attempt to fight a large fire.

Active Threat

  1. RUN: If you can safely exit the building, do so immediately. Take patients with you if possible. Leave belongings behind. Call 911 once you are safe.
  2. HIDE: If you cannot exit safely, lock and barricade the door. Turn off lights. Silence phones. Stay away from windows and doors. Remain quiet.
  3. FIGHT: As an absolute last resort, if you are in immediate danger and cannot run or hide, use whatever is available to defend yourself.
  4. When law enforcement arrives: Show empty hands. Follow all instructions. Do not make sudden movements. Do not point or yell.
